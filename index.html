ESPAÑA

Más vale tarde que nunca
Eleven gems from Iberia

La España de Postal

“Sangría. Sun. Tapas. A bloke with a West Ham Forever tattoo. Two euros for a glass of red.”

The Spain on the postcard. The Spain of summer holidays and Costa del Sol. For most of the 20th century, this was Spain’s wine reputation too. Generous, cheerful, inexpensive, drunk by the litre. Vino de mesa.

Then about thirty years ago, a few things happened. People started taking the old vines seriously. Producers stopped trying to make Spanish wine taste French. Foreign critics noticed.

Tonight is not the Spain on the postcard.

Une Confession

“I love French wine. I love Italian wine. Spain… I always looked down on Spain.”

A confession before we begin: I love French wine. I love Italian wine almost as much. I love both expensively, uncritically, and to the slight detriment of my wallet.

Spain has, for most of my drinking life, been the country I quietly looked down on. The French invented the AOC. The Italians have more native grape varieties than the rest of Europe combined. Spain spent decades selling wine by the litre and pretending that was fine.

It wasn’t fine. They’ve been quietly fixing it. Tonight, reluctantly, I’m here to say so.

Y Sin Embargo

“More vines than France. Older vines than France. Grapes France doesn’t have.”

Spain has more land under vine than any country on earth. Many of its vines are ungrafted; phylloxera arrived late and missed several regions entirely. Hundreds of indigenous grapes nobody else grows.

The French make wine like architects. The Spanish make wine like cooks. By feel, by tradition, with whatever the vineyard happened to give them that year. Eleven wines tonight. Catalonia to the Canaries, Galicia to Andalucía. None of them French. All of them serious.

PARADA 1

Penedès, Corpinnat
Recaredo Subtil Brut Nature 2019
Grapes: 100% Xarel·lo
Corpinnat · Biodynamic · Organic (CCPAE)
Hand-disgorged Zero dosage
“Creamy texture, fine integrated bubbles. White fruits, white flowers, autolytic notes. Aged in old 300L oak.”
Don’t call this cava. In 2019, Recaredo and a handful of other Penedès estates walked out of the Cava DO because cava had become a supermarket category. Corpinnat is what they make instead. Hand-disgorged. Second fermentation with their own free-run must rather than cane sugar. The technique is borrowed from Champagne. Champagne, for the record, hasn’t been hand-disgorging for decades.

PARADA 2

Somontano, Pyrenees Foothills
Pirineos Gewürztraminer 2024
Grapes: 100% Gewürztraminer
DO Somontano · Aragón
13% ABV Pyrenees fruit
“Lychee, passion fruit, ripe peach. Rose petal and jasmine. A hint of spice. Surprisingly fresh for the variety.”
Gewürztraminer in Spain is, frankly, an act of taxonomic mischief. Somontano sits on the Spanish side of the Pyrenees, but the climate is closer to Alsace than to Andalusia. Cool nights, mountain altitude, granite-influenced soils. The grape behaves itself in a way it sometimes doesn’t even in Alsace. The Spanish have no real ancestral claim to the variety. They just made it well anyway.

Glass #3

PARADA 3

Rueda, Castilla y León
Martinsancho Verdejo 2024
Producer: Ángel Rodríguez Vidal
Grapes: 100% Verdejo · bush vines, 40+ years
DO Rueda · Organic methods
Vineyard since 1784
“Straw yellow. Tropical and ripe fruit, mountain herb, a hint of fennel. Fresh, fruity, beautifully balanced.”
In the early 1970s, growers in Rueda were ripping out Verdejo to plant something, anything, more profitable. Ángel Rodríguez refused. His Martinsancho vineyard had been in the family since 1784, which is roughly when France was starting to take wine seriously. He replanted his other plots from cuttings. Today most Verdejo in Rueda traces back to those vines. A man saved an entire grape variety. France would have made a documentary about him. Spain made this wine.
?

PARADA 4

Tenerife, Valle de la Orotava
Tajinaste Listán Blanco 2024
Grapes: 90% Listán Blanco, 10% Albillo
DOP Islas Canarias · Volcanic, ungrafted
Pico del Teide slopes
“Light straw with green reflections. Slightly smoky nose, floral, pineapple, apple, fennel. Fresh palate, balanced acidity.”
Phylloxera couldn’t be bothered with the Canaries. The vines here are ungrafted, planted on volcanic ash, on the slopes of an active volcano, on the wrong side of the Atlantic. They’re trained on a system called cordón trenzado: three vines literally braided together, unique to these islands. France has, over the centuries, devised many sophisticated viticultural techniques. None of them involve plaiting.

PARADA 5

Rías Baixas, O Rosal, Galicia
Terras Gauda Caiño La Mar 2022
Grapes: 97% Caiño Blanco, 3% Albariño & Loureiro
DO Rías Baixas · Organic, slate soils
Saved from extinction
“Peach, melon, pineapple, persimmon, lychee. Mineral and salty notes. Full-bodied yet delicate, deep, silky, with great structure.”
Everyone knows Albariño. Almost nobody knows Caiño Blanco, an heirloom Galician variety that Terras Gauda rescued from extinction. Grown on slate rather than the granite Galicia usually plays with, which is why La Mar tastes like the Atlantic in a way Albariño rarely manages. The total number of producers making wine from this grape is around twelve. None of them are French. Worth noting.
?

PARADA 6

Ribera del Duero, Old Castile
Alfredo Maestro “Lovamor” Albillo 2023
Grapes: 100% Albillo Mayor
Style: Skin-contact orange
Castilla y León · 60-120 yr old vines
Wild yeast No additions
“Pear drop, banana, peach and apricot stone. Honeysuckle, white lilac. Orange tang and herbs. Texture, minerality, warm fruit.”
An orange wine from Ribera del Duero, a region built on serious Tempranillo, where this sort of thing is generally regarded as eccentric. Albillo Mayor is the local white grape, traditionally added in trace amounts to soften red blends. Alfredo Maestro takes it on its own. A week on the skins, wild yeast, nothing added, nothing removed. The French wine establishment is still locked in spirited debate about whether orange wine ought to exist. The Spanish appear to have moved on without them.
?

PARADA 7

Sierra de Gredos, Madrid
Comando G “Rozas” Tinto 2023
Producers: Fernando García & Dani Landi
Grapes: 100% Garnacha · 90 year old vines
Vinos de Madrid · Granite · 900m altitude
Whole bunch Foudre & concrete
“Liveliness, lightness, the fruit of the Mediterranean harvest. Garnacha as Pinot Noir wishes it could be.”
Garnacha. Or, as they say in Châteauneuf, “Grenache, which we obviously invented.” The Spanish gave the French the grape. The French took it, put it on a French label, and quietly stopped mentioning where it came from. Then in 2008, Fernando García and Dani Landi started Comando G in the Sierra de Gredos. G for Garnacha, Granite, Gredos. Old vines at 900 metres, whole-bunch fermentation, glorious wines. Châteauneuf is now paying very polite, very nervous attention.
?

PARADA 8

Rioja Alta, Villalba de Rioja
Pedrajo Suañé Tinto Reserva 2020
Grapes: 100% Tempranillo
Rioja DOCa · B-Corp · 24 months French oak
Concrete & barrel 15+ months bottle
“Garnet red, dense and deep. Black fruit, plum stone, fig bread, subtle wood, liquorice. Fresh, silky tannin, long finish.”
“Suañé” is the Spanish phonetic spelling of the French soigné: refined, careful, well-tended. Two friends, Alberto Pedrajo and Javier Alonso, started this estate in 2013 with vineyards at the high northeast of Rioja Alta. They named their wine after a French word, aged it in French oak, and then proceeded to make a Tempranillo the French could not have made. Take it as flattery.

PARADA 9

Priorat, Catalonia
Hodgkinson Garnacha Peluda 2019
Grapes: 100% Garnacha Peluda
Style: 18 months in 500L French oak
DOQ Priorat · Llicorella slate · 1978 plot
Wild yeast 15.5% ABV
“Power and lift. The slate of Priorat carries through everything. Higher acidity than standard Garnacha. It’s the ‘peluda’ (hairy) variant.”
In the early 1970s, an English architect bought land in Priorat, back when Priorat was best known for not being Rioja. He married a French woman. Their son Luca did his oenology at Bordeaux, then worked harvests in Hermitage, Saint-Émilion, Tuscany, Stellenbosch, Porto, Mendoza, and Chile before coming home. Garnacha Peluda is the “hairy” mutation of Garnacha. Smaller berries, white fuzz under the leaves, higher acidity. The wine is unmistakably Priorat. The Bordeaux training shows. The slate does the rest.
?

PARADA 10

Rioja, Cuzcurrita
Urbina Gran Reserva Especial 2001
Grapes: 95% Tempranillo, 5% Mazuelo & Graciano
Rioja DOCa · 24m American oak · 48m bottle
24 years old Family since 1870
“Black fruit, leather, tobacco. Layers of savoury blackcurrant. Velvet smoothness, great finesse, excellent length.”
Twenty-four years old. Bottled when nobody owned a smartphone and Spain still used pesetas, then held back at the bodega until they decided it was ready. The Urbinas have been making Rioja in Cuzcurrita since 1870. Five generations of refusing to sell wine before it’s finished. Most Bordeaux estates abandoned this discipline three decades ago. The Spanish, true to form, never noticed they were supposed to.
?

PARADA 11

Jerez, Andalucía
Hidalgo Marqués de Rodil Palo Cortado
Grapes: 100% Palomino
Average age: 20+ years · Solera since 1860
DO Jerez · Singular Wine, no added sulphites
17% ABV 5th gen family
“Bright mahogany. Intense nutty nose, dry, salty, delicate yet rich. The savoury character of Amontillado with the power of Oloroso.”
Palo cortado is the unicorn of sherries. A barrel begins life as a fino, ageing under flor, the yeast veil that gives sherry its salt. Then, on certain barrels, for reasons no one fully understands, the flor dies. The cellar master tastes them, marks them with a single diagonal slash, and ages them oxidatively for another two decades. The wine in your glass is from a solera Hidalgo has been running since 1860. France has nothing comparable to sherry. The French have made their peace with this.
